Festivals

Festivals in Bagan are deeply rooted in Buddhist traditions and local culture.
Each celebration brings together communities and visitors through music, food and light

Lighting Festival

The Lighting Festival is called Thadingyut in Burmese. It is celebrated nationally at the end of the Buddhist Lent in October. During the festival night, homes, temples, and pagodas across Bagan and the entire country are illuminated with thousands of candles, lanterns, and oil lamps to honor the Buddha’s descent from heaven. It is a time for families to pay respect to their elders and make offerings at monasteries. People also give gifts to their loved ones. Beautiful fireworks and lantern releases at pagodas make the celebration even more special. The festival creates a vibrant atmosphere filled with lights, music, and traditional snacks.

SHWEZIGON FESTIVAL

The Shwezigon Festival is a yearly festival celebrated on the full moon day of Tazaungmone (in November). It is one of the most famous and busiest festivals in Bagan and has been celebrated since the Bagan dynasty. The festival lasts for 15 days and remains a deeply rooted tradition in Myanmar’s cultural and religious life. It is held around the Shwezigon Pagoda, a significant Buddhist shrine in Bagan. Pilgrims from across Myanmar gather to offer prayers, make donations, and take part in the ritual of presenting alms bowls filled with offerings to over a thousand monks and novices. The festival also features traditional music and dance performances with market stalls where locals sell lacquerware, glazed pots, handwoven cotton blankets, local crafts, food, and household items.

WATER FESTIVAL

The Water Festival, also known as Thingyan, is a 4–5 day celebration of the Burmese New Year. It is held in mid-April during the summer season. The festival is a time for cleansing bad luck and misfortunes from the previous year and welcoming the new year with a fresh, joyous start. The splashing of water symbolizes washing away sins and impurities from the past year. This celebration is held throughout Myanmar with concerts, traditional performances, and various cultural activities. While it is a joyous festival, there are cultural sensitivities: it is not appropriate to splash water on monks, the elderly, pregnant women, or babies.
