Customs & Community

Festivals

Festivals in Bagan are deeply rooted in Buddhist traditions and local culture.
Each celebration brings together communities and visitors through music, food and light

Rows of candles are lit to celebrate the Thadingyut Lighting Festival, marking the end of Buddhist Lent

Lighting Festival

The Lighting Festival is called Thadingyut in Burmese. It is celebrated nationally at the end of the Buddhist Lent in October. During the festival night, homes, temples, and pagodas across Bagan and the entire country are illuminated with thousands of candles, lanterns, and oil lamps to honor the Buddha’s descent from heaven. It is a time for families to pay respect to their elders and make offerings at monasteries. People also give gifts to their loved ones. Beautiful fireworks and lantern releases at pagodas make the celebration even more special. The festival creates a vibrant atmosphere filled with lights, music, and traditional snacks.

SHWEZIGON FESTIVAL

The Shwezigon Festival is a yearly festival celebrated on the full moon day of Tazaungmone (in November). It is one of the most famous and busiest festivals in Bagan and has been celebrated since the Bagan dynasty. The festival lasts for 15 days and remains a deeply rooted tradition in Myanmar’s cultural and religious life. It is held around the Shwezigon Pagoda, a significant Buddhist shrine in Bagan. Pilgrims from across Myanmar gather to offer prayers, make donations, and take part in the ritual of presenting alms bowls filled with offerings to over a thousand monks and novices. The festival also features traditional music and dance performances with market stalls where locals sell lacquerware, glazed pots, handwoven cotton blankets, local crafts, food, and household items.

WATER FESTIVAL

The Water Festival, also known as Thingyan, is a 4–5 day celebration of the Burmese New Year. It is held in mid-April during the summer season. The festival is a time for cleansing bad luck and misfortunes from the previous year and welcoming the new year with a fresh, joyous start. The splashing of water symbolizes washing away sins and impurities from the past year. This celebration is held throughout Myanmar with concerts, traditional performances, and various cultural activities. While it is a joyous festival, there are cultural sensitivities: it is not appropriate to splash water on monks, the elderly, pregnant women, or babies.

Cultural Dance

Bagan Period Dance on Youtube by Sai Zayar

The traditional cultural dances of Bagan are based on Bagan dynasty and are an significant role in Myanmar's heritage. These perfomances are held during festivals, ceremonies, special celebrations to showcase graceful movements, unique custom and music inspired by Buddhist legends and folklore. This is Bagan Period Dance, which tells stories from ancient times through symbolic hand gestures and elegant choreography. Dancers wear beautifully embroidered costumes and ornate headdresses, reflecting the style of the royal courts back in the days.

Nowadays, cultural dance performances can still be enjoyed at the festivals and cultural shows around Bagan. This offers visitors an insight to the traditions that have been preserved for centuries.

Take off your shoes before entering temples

It is believed that the foot is the most impure part of the body. Burmese people and tourists will have remove shoes at the place of temple entry. The same goes for entering Burmese homes if you are invited, you should leave your shoes at the door. You should never point your toes towards a Buddha statue or at someone or anything. 

Public Displays of Affection

In order to honour Burmese culture, couples should refrain from publicly displaying their affection. Avoid kissing on the street or in a restaurant to avoid startling or upsetting the locals.

Dress code

We advise all visitors to respect Burma's culture by dressing appropriately. Wearing a Burmese longyi—a piece of cloth that encircles your waist—is preferable, mainly when visiting monasteries or temples.

Thanaka – Traditional Face Cosmetic

Thanaka is a yellowish-white cosmetic paste made from grounded bark of thanaka tree( its botanical name Limonia acidissima). It is widely worn by Burmese women, men, and children on the face and arms. Thanaka serves as a natural sunscreen, provides a cooling effect, and is part of Myanmar's cultural identity.

Tea Leaf Salad – Lahpet Thoke

Tea Leaf Salad, Lahpet Thoke in Burmese, is a famous Burmese dish made with fermented tea leaves, peanuts, sesame seeds, garlic, lime, and other crunchy toppings. It is considered a symbol of hospitality and is traditionally served when guests arrive.
